# LinkedIn Post #1 — Agents vs Copilots

**Tone:** Direct, opinionated, no-fluff. You're a CS leader who has actually built this stuff, not a thought-leader recycling Gartner takes.

**Hook strategy:** Open with a contrarian or uncomfortable observation. Make people stop scrolling.

---

## Version A — Contrarian Hook (recommended)

Most "AI for Customer Success" demos I've seen in the last 6 months aren't agents.

They're copilots in a trench coat.

The difference matters more than the marketing wants you to think:

— A **copilot** sits next to your CSM. Drafts the email. Summarizes the call. Suggests the next step. Your CSM still has to act.

— An **agent** sits in the workflow. Detects the churn signal. Sends the email. Books the QBR. Updates the CRM. No human in the loop unless something exceeds its confidence threshold.

One makes your CSM 20% faster. The other lets you cover 10x more accounts.

Both are useful. But if you're a Head of CS being told "our AI agent handles renewals" and what you're seeing is a draft button — you're being sold a copilot at agent pricing.

The test I use when vendors pitch me:

1. Can it act without human approval on a defined trigger?
2. Does it write back to systems of record (CRM, billing, ticketing)?
3. Does it have a confidence threshold + escalation path?

If the answer to all three isn't yes, it's a copilot. Price it accordingly.

I wrote up the full breakdown — including how to spot the difference in a demo and what each is actually worth — here: https://www.onboard-success.com/playbooks/agents-vs-copilots

What's your test for separating the two?

Last quarter a vendor told me their "AI agent" would autonomously handle renewals.

In the demo, what I actually saw was:

→ A nice UI that flagged at-risk accounts
→ A button that drafted a renewal email
→ A "review and send" workflow

That's not an agent. That's a copilot with better marketing.

## Posting notes

- **Best time to post:** Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10am CET (your audience is mostly Europe + East Coast US morning)
- **First 2 hours matter most** — reply to every comment in the first hour
- **Use the link at the end** but DON'T put it in the first comment trick — LinkedIn's algorithm has learned that pattern and it kills reach now in 2026
- **No hashtags** — they don't help anymore, sometimes hurt
- **Tag nobody** unless they're genuinely part of the story

## What to do after posting

1. Wait 90 min — check engagement
2. If it's getting traction (>20 reactions in first 2h): start engaging in CS-adjacent posts that day, same audience
3. If it's flat: don't delete. Move on. Post #2 in 3 days.
4. Track in `memory/`: views, reactions, comments, comment quality (real conversations vs "Great post!")
